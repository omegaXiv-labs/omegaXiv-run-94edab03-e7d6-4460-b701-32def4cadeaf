# Literature Overview: Biased-Noise Surface-Code Thresholds Under Realistic Gate Sets

This overview distills the 35-source corpus in `knowledge/refs.jsonl` and `knowledge/notes.md` into a comparison framework for surface-code threshold studies under dephasing-biased and gate-dependent noise. The immediate target is not just asymptotic threshold folklore, but the narrower question posed by the project brief: what the literature implies for repeated syndrome-extraction circuits, realistic scheduling, and finite-distance logical-error estimates at `d in {3,5,7,9}` for bias ratios `eta in {1,5,20,50}`.

The literature separates naturally into five categories. First are the foundational threshold and topological-code papers, including [dennis2002_topological_memory], [wang2003_confinement_higgs], and [kitaev2003_anyons]. These papers define the homological decoding picture and the statistical-mechanics interpretation of thresholds. Second are early bias-aware fault-tolerance papers such as [aliferis2008_biased_noise] and [stephens2013_topological_biased], which argue that dephasing bias should be treated as exploitable structure rather than averaged into an unbiased Pauli rate. Third are tailored surface-code families and deformations, spanning [tuckett2018_ultrahigh_threshold], [tuckett2019_tailoring_surface_codes], [tuckett2020_fault_tolerant_thresholds], [li2019_compass_codes], [bonilla2021_xzzx], [xu2023_tailored_xzzx], and [dua2024_clifford_deformed]. Fourth are studies centered on realistic noise, decoder quality, and schedule effects, including [bravyi2014_mld_surface], [darmawan2018_tensor_network], [higgott2023_improved_decoding], [tsai2024_temporal_fragility], [martinez2025_two_level_circuit], and [chubb2021_correlated_noise]. Fifth are tooling and implementation sources such as [gidney2021_stim_paper], [higgott2025_sparse_blossom], [stim_repo], [pymatching_repo], and [qiskit_aer_docs], which matter because the practical credibility of a threshold estimate depends on whether the simulation and decoding stack can support the intended parameter sweep.

Across these categories, there is a strong consensus on one structural point: threshold is not a single number attached to “the surface code.” It is an emergent property of a code family, a decoder, a circuit schedule, and a noise model. The foundational papers [dennis2002_topological_memory] and [wang2003_confinement_higgs] formalize threshold through asymptotic suppression of logical failure and phase-transition analogies. Their assumptions are broad: local stochastic noise, repeated syndrome extraction, and large-distance scaling. Those assumptions remain conceptually central, but later biased-noise papers repeatedly show that the asymptotic picture can mislead when the operating point is small-distance and circuit-level. This is the first major gap between old theory and current practice.

The literature is also unified by a second consensus claim: dephasing bias is a resource if the entire error-correction stack preserves it. [aliferis2008_biased_noise] makes this argument before the modern tailored surface-code wave, showing that threshold improvements follow when fault-tolerant gadgets align with the dominant error type. [stephens2013_topological_biased] brings the same logic directly into surface-code syndrome extraction and shows that bias-aware use of the surface code can raise thresholds relative to standard unbiased baselines. [tuckett2020_fault_tolerant_thresholds], [bonilla2021_xzzx], and [martinez2025_two_level_circuit] all reinforce the same point from different angles: the relevant question is not whether bias exists, but whether the chosen code deformation, measurement cycle, and gate set keep that bias visible at the detector level.

Where the corpus becomes more differentiated is in the mechanism used to exploit bias. [tuckett2018_ultrahigh_threshold] and [tuckett2019_tailoring_surface_codes] argue that carefully tailored surface-code geometries can drive thresholds dramatically higher under strong Z-dominant noise. [li2019_compass_codes] broadens this into a deformation framework, treating code geometry and stabilizer organization as tunable design parameters. [bonilla2021_xzzx] then becomes the dominant modern reference because it gives a particularly clean deformation, the XZZX stabilizer pattern, and ties that deformation to explicit scaling claims. In the stored corpus, its key equations include `k = O(1)` encoded qubits on `n = O(d^2)` physical qubits and logical-failure scaling `O((p / sqrt(eta))^(d/2))`, with `O(p^(d^2/2))` in the infinite-bias limit. The implication is methodological as much as theoretical: comparisons based only on threshold crossing miss an important part of the story, because bias-tailored codes can improve subthreshold slope and finite-distance logical-error behavior even when threshold shifts are modest.

At the same time, the sources do not present an entirely smooth narrative of improvement. There is a clear contradiction, or at least a tension, between idealized biased-noise gains and realistic circuit-level performance. [tuckett2018_ultrahigh_threshold] and [bonilla2021_xzzx] are often remembered for very large gains. But [tuckett2020_fault_tolerant_thresholds], [higgott2023_improved_decoding], [tsai2024_temporal_fragility], and [martinez2025_two_level_circuit] show that those gains depend on details that are easy to understate in broad summaries. [higgott2023_improved_decoding] identifies fragile boundaries and decoder limitations as concrete failure modes. [tsai2024_temporal_fragility] shows that time-direction effects and schedule order can erode bias advantages in biased-noise XY surface-code variants. [martinez2025_two_level_circuit] is especially important because it shifts the question away from ideal bias-preserving hardware and toward ordinary two-level qubits with imperfectly preserved bias. The contradiction is not that earlier high-threshold claims are wrong; it is that they live under assumptions that realistic gate decompositions may violate.

This is exactly where decoder methodology enters. The literature repeatedly says that decoder choice is not a secondary implementation detail. [bravyi2014_mld_surface] shows why maximum-likelihood-like decoding changes the apparent threshold and the quality of finite-size scaling. [darmawan2018_tensor_network] extends this lesson to realistic surface-code noise, showing that richer simulation and decoding methods are needed once the noise model becomes gate dependent or correlated. [higgott2025_sparse_blossom] shifts the emphasis from asymptotic decoding quality to throughput, arguing that broad Monte Carlo sweeps are only practical when detector graphs can be decoded at very high speed. The practical consensus is straightforward: if the circuit can be compiled into a detector error model, Stim plus PyMatching or sparse blossom is the most natural baseline. If the target noise model requires more custom channel composition than that workflow easily exposes, [qiskit_aer_docs] becomes the main fallback.

The corpus is unusually strong on finite-size warnings, and those warnings are directly relevant to the requested distances `d in {3,5,7,9}`. [robertson2017_small_memories] already cautions that finite-distance preference ordering can differ from asymptotic threshold ordering. [xiao2024_finite_size_corrections] is the clearest formal statement of this issue. In the stored notes it is associated with the parameterization `p_x = p_y = p_z / (2 eta)` and `p = p_x + p_y + p_z`, together with a special point `p = (1 + eta^(-1)) / (2 + eta^(-1))` and the finite-size expression `P_f = 3/4 - (1/4) exp(-2 d_Z arctanh(1/(2 eta)))`. Even without relying on the full derivation, the message is clear: apparent threshold crossings at small distance can be distorted by finite-size corrections and even anti-threshold behavior. This means that any planned comparison between analytical threshold expectations and Monte Carlo curves should report both crossing-based threshold estimates and raw logical error per round at each distance. Otherwise, the study risks labeling finite-size artifacts as physical contradictions.

The assumptions behind the literature also diverge in ways that matter for this project. Foundational threshold papers assume local stochastic Pauli noise and asymptotic scaling. XZZX and tailored-code papers often assume strongly biased Pauli channels with favorable boundary conditions and decoders. Correlated-noise analysis in [chubb2021_correlated_noise] relaxes the iid assumption and shows that threshold-critical points can shift materially when correlations are included. Circuit-level papers such as [tuckett2020_fault_tolerant_thresholds] and [martinez2025_two_level_circuit] assume repeated noisy syndrome extraction and hardware-constrained gate sets. These assumption differences explain many of the apparent contradictions in reported thresholds. A 5% or higher circuit-level threshold under a tailored schedule is not directly comparable to a code-capacity threshold under idealized iid bias, and neither is directly comparable to a gate-dependent two-level-qubit result where the circuit only partially preserves bias.

The broader research frontier shows that the current project is still operating inside a larger design space. [huang2023_3d_topological_codes] extends tailoring logic to 3D topological codes. [dua2024_clifford_deformed] argues that XZZX is only one point in a wider local-Clifford deformation landscape. [liang2025_xyz_cyclic] suggests further gains are possible beyond standard surface-code-like families. [setiawan2025_x3z3_floquet] pushes the idea that schedule design itself can be the tailoring knob, not merely the stabilizer layout. These papers do not change the core plan for the present study, but they matter when describing limitations: even a careful evaluation of standard and XZZX surface codes under biased noise would not exhaust the current state of the art.

Taken together, the literature supports several grounded conclusions. First, there is genuine consensus that dephasing bias can substantially improve performance, but only if code design, schedule design, and decoder design are aligned. Second, there is also consensus that realistic circuit-level threshold estimates are lower fidelity when they ignore correlated faults, fragile boundaries, or time-like error propagation. Third, there is no consensus that asymptotically best code families are automatically best at the finite distances most likely to matter in near-term simulations. Fourth, the methodological gap between analytical theory and practical Monte Carlo remains open: theory papers tend to speak in asymptotic or stylized finite-size language, while simulation papers emphasize schedule- and decoder-specific performance on concrete circuits.

Those gaps point directly to the open problems for the next phase. The first open problem is calibration: how much of the threshold gain predicted by XZZX and related tailored constructions survives when the actual gate decomposition only partially preserves bias? The second is interpretability: when threshold curves disagree with theoretical expectations, how much of the mismatch is finite-size correction, how much is decoder suboptimality, and how much is the circuit schedule itself? The third is model mismatch: can a single simulation workflow capture both the clean detector-error-model regime favored by Stim and the more hardware-faithful gate-dependent channels naturally expressed in Qiskit Aer? The fourth is fairness of comparison: what counts as a valid literature baseline when code-capacity, circuit-level, and hardware-constrained results mix different assumptions?

For the user’s target study, the primary comparison set should therefore be [tuckett2020_fault_tolerant_thresholds], [bonilla2021_xzzx], [higgott2023_improved_decoding], [xiao2024_finite_size_corrections], and [martinez2025_two_level_circuit], with [chubb2021_correlated_noise] as the main reminder that realistic gate-dependent bias may induce correlations that simple iid theory misses. The main methodological consensus is strong enough to guide implementation: simulate repeated syndrome extraction rather than code-capacity noise alone, expose schedule and boundary choices as explicit experiment parameters, report finite-distance logical error per round in addition to threshold crossings, and document clearly where the chosen simulator and decoder depart from the assumptions of the comparison papers.
